Performance Levels (PL) and Safety Integrity Levels (SIL)

PL and SIL (if applied to machinery control systems), are both expressed in Probability of Failure per Hour (PFH). Both ratings also have requirements concerning structure, the degree of self-detection built into a device, and the confidence that design faults have been avoided.

The main difference between PL and SIL lies in the parameters that express those requirements. The PL not only has a PFH, but also a reliability value for each channel inside the safety-related system. This is called the Mean Time To dangerous Failure (MTTFd) of a channel.

The Safety Integrity Level (SIL) is used to specify the integrity requirements of the safety functions to be allocated to the Electrical / Electronic / Programmable Electronic (E / E / PE) safety-related systems.

Performance Levels are only referenced in one standard, ISO 13849, however this is the pivotal standard as it is harmonised to the Machinery Directive. Safety Integrity Levels are referenced in IEC 61508 and all the other standards derived from it.

The ability of Safety-Related Parts of Control Systems (SRP/CS) to perform a safety function under foreseeable conditions is allocated one of five Performance Levels (PL). These performance levels are defined in terms of probability of dangerous failure per hour.

To facilitate the assessment of achieved PL, EN ISO 13849-1 categorises control system structures into one of five classes of design architecture (B, 1, 2, 3, or 4) according to specific design criteria and specified behaviours under fault conditions as shown in the table below.

To fulfil the requirements of category B The SRP/CS has to be designed, constructed, selected, assembled and combined in accordance with the relevant European standards (EN) and using basic safety principles for the specific application to withstand the expected operating stresses, however a single component (sub-unit) fault can lead to the loss of the safety function.

Category 4 SRP/CS has a much greater level of reliability due to its dual channel architecture (redundancy) and fault monitoring systems. Category 4 SRP/CS are designed so that:

  • a single fault in any of these safety-related parts does not lead to a loss of the safety function
  • the single fault is detected at or before the next demand upon the safety functions, e.g. immediately, at switch on, or at end of a machine operating cycle … but …
  • if this detection is not possible, then an accumulation of undetected faults shall not lead to the loss of the safety function.
